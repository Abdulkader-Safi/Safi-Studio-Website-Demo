# Cut Square

> The visual philosophy behind the Safi Studio mark.

## The movement

Cut Square is the discipline of the command line made into a logo. It treats a brand mark the way a senior engineer treats a function: nothing decorative, nothing that doesn't carry weight, every edge placed on purpose. The mark is built from one idea, the bracket, the symbol a developer types a hundred times a day to open and own a block of code. Safi Studio is built on copy-and-own, taking the code into your own hands, so the identity is a pair of brackets closing around a single letter. You hold the S. You own it.

## Form and space

The mark lives on a grid and obeys it. A square field, hairline-bordered, with sharp two-pixel corners that never soften into the friendly radius every generated logo defaults to. Inside, the S is constructed, not drawn: cut from straight strokes and clean joints so it reads as something machined rather than handwritten. Two bracket marks frame it, left and right, the way a terminal prompt frames a cursor. The negative space is as deliberate as the ink. Generous, quiet, measured to the same rhythm as the rest of the system.

## Color and material

The palette is warm paper and ink, never the cold default of a tech logo. A bone ground, near-black ink, and one accent of clay vermilion used once and used well, on the bracket or the cursor, the single point of heat in an otherwise calm field. The mark must survive inversion: ink on paper, paper on ink, and a single flat colour for stamps and favicons. Restraint is the material. The accent earns its place by being rare.

## Scale and rhythm

A logo is judged at sixteen pixels and at two metres, and this one is built to hold at both. The strokes carry a consistent weight so the S never thins to nothing in a favicon or bloats in a header. The wordmark sits beside the mark in a grotesque display cut with real character, paired with a monospace tag that nods to the terminal without shouting. The lockup breathes on one baseline, mark and word locked to a shared optical centre, the spacing tuned by eye until it sits still.

## Craft

This is meticulously crafted work, the product of deep expertise, every curve and corner labored over until it looks inevitable. The kind of mark that reads as obvious only because someone at the top of their field removed everything that wasn't. Master-level execution means it works in one colour, holds at any size, and carries the same point of view as the site it belongs to. Painstaking attention to the millimetre, so the result feels machined, owned, and entirely its own.
